Explanation (if you want it) ▽

For this project, I took words and phrases, translated them into shapes and numbers using secret codes (some straightforward, some very difficult to solve, and others totally unsolvable, e.g., because I intentionally made mistakes that cannot be traced back to the original text), and then displayed the resulting patterns in sewn fabric and projected animations. These indecipherable codes are a method of simultaneously expressing myself in words and hiding behind code.

Hiding a concept in code and then turning the result into a large piece of fabric that hangs on a wall is a way of hiding in plain sight. The animations that flicker between numbers and letters show a dynamic version of the interplay between the hidden (numbers) and the expressed (letters).

The process of saying something inexpressible, making it into something that is indecipherable, and then turning it into something physical – by cutting fabric, sewing the pieces together, and hanging it up – transforms a thing (idea) that was originally too scary to say into just a thing (object) on a wall. Once it's become an object, the idea is less frightening to present; the emotional thing has been said in the course of my working through it physically. Regardless of whether the final result is understood by an audience, the process of creating and displaying it is fulfilling for me.




9dk53d07o ("I don't know")

When Spanish colonists encountered the Akimel O'odham people in the early 1600s in what is now the Southwest United States, they called them the “Pima Indians” based on the sound of the Akimel O'odham phrase for “I don't know.” The European imposition of the phrase “I don't know” as a name for the Akimel O'odham people is an undisguised metaphor for the imposition of assumed ignorance of the colonized by the colonizer. Labelling Native peoples as ignorant and primitive is way by which colonizers attempt to justify colonization.

The pattern of the textile is inspired by the pattern of the Akimel O'odham bowl and the phrase “I don't know” encoded as a number in base 23 and then converted to base 25. The numbers and letters projected on the textile are an excerpt from the Akimel O'odham creation story, which tells of Juhwertamahkai, who created, disliked, and then destroyed humankind multiple times until satisfied with the result.

Beyond the conversation around the relationship between European colonists and the Akimel O'od- ham people, the phrase “I don't know” speaks to me on a personal level. As I embark on an artistic journey, I feel like I don't know much about anything. I am interested in how a state of “I don't know” can be imposed on someone else, or self-imposed despite actual expertise or knowledge. I have been embracing the process of making attempts to create, tearing it all down, and then attempting again, parallel to Juhwertamahkai's creation of humanity.




Passacaglia

A passacaglia is a form of music composition in which a repeating ostinato is overlaid with variations on a theme, similar to the quilt structure of a static pattern overlaid with a dynamic animation. The word “passacaglia” evokes the concept of “passing on the street” and the text in the animation is a poem about an encounter with a person on the street. The quilt pattern takes the string “passacaglia” from base 9 to base 19, representing the date of the encounter. The poem is translated from base 2 to base 4, a representation of a rejection of binaries in favor of a more expansive understanding of the world.
